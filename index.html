東京
Week One · Aug 1–7Tokyo
Day1

Land & Ease In

Shinjuku by night · Sat Aug 1

Arrival day, so the only goal is staying awake until a normal bedtime to beat the jet lag. Land in the afternoon, drop the bags, then wander Shinjuku as the neon comes on.

  • KabukichoTokyo's biggest entertainment quarter, all neon and noise. The giant Godzilla head roars over the Toho building.
    Omoide YokochoA cramped, lantern-lit alley of tiny yakitori counters, smoke and beer since the postwar years.
    Golden GaiSix narrow lanes packed with around 200 pocket-sized bars, each seating just a handful. Pure atmosphere.
    Metro Government BuildingFree observation decks on the 45th floor, 202m up, with a wide city view and Mt. Fuji on a clear day. Costs nothing.
    Shinjuku GyoenA huge, calm landscaped park a few minutes away when the neon gets to be too much.
    Depachika food hallsThe basement food floors of Isetan and the big department stores, a wonderland of takeaway treats.

    Good to know

    • The station is a maze, pick an exit number and follow the signs to it.
    • Neon looks best after dark, so save the wander for evening.
    • The Government Building deck is genuinely free, no ticket.
  • Yakitori skewersChicken grilled every way, order momo (thigh), negima (with scallion), and tsukune (meatball) to start.
    Motsu nikomiA rich, slow-simmered offal-and-miso stew. More adventurous, very local.
    A cold oneGrab a beer or a highball, sit at the counter, and watch the grill work.
    The alley itselfRed lanterns, hanging noren curtains, and steam, one of the most photogenic corners of Tokyo.

    Good to know

    • Go early evening before the counters fill up.
    • Most stalls seat six to eight people, so splitting up is normal.
    • Bring cash, many spots do not take cards.
  • ShoyuTokyo's home style, a clear soy-based broth. Clean and classic.
    TonkotsuRich, creamy pork-bone broth from Kyushu. The crowd-pleaser.
    MisoHearty and a little sweet, made for cold nights, born in Sapporo.
    TsukemenThick noodles served dry with a concentrated dipping broth on the side. Dip and slurp.
    IchiranFamous chain where you eat in a private booth and never see the staff. Great first bowl.

    Good to know

    • Many shops order through a vending machine at the door, buy a ticket, hand it over.
    • Slurping is encouraged, it cools the noodles and is a compliment.
    • Tokyo Station's Ramen Street lines up eight top shops in one basement.
Keep it light · then crash
Day2

Old Tokyo Meets New

Asakusa + Skytree · Sun Aug 2

A temple from the 600s in the morning, a futuristic tower in the afternoon — old and new Tokyo in a single day.

  • KaminarimonThe Thunder Gate and its four-meter red lantern, the classic Tokyo photo.
    Nakamise-doriThe shopping street to the temple, snacks and souvenirs for 250 meters.
    Hozomon GateThe second, larger gate, with giant straw sandals hung on the back.
    Main Hall & PagodaThe great hall and a five-story pagoda. Waft the incense smoke over you for luck.
    Omikuji fortunesShake a numbered stick for a paper fortune. Tie a bad one to the rack and leave it behind.

    Good to know

    • Come early morning to beat the crowds and the heat.
    • It's free to visit.
    • The Sumida River and Skytree are a short walk behind the temple.
  • Nakamise-doriThe centuries-old shopping street, fresh rice crackers and warm bean cakes.
    Kappabashi StreetKitchen-tool row, where restaurants buy their gear and the famous plastic food samples.
    Sumida River cruiseHop a boat down the river toward the bay for a different view of the city.
    Hoppy-doriA lively alley of cheap eats and drinks, great for an afternoon snack crawl.
    Rickshaw rideA local puller will run you through the backstreets and point out the history.

    Good to know

    • Pair it with Senso-ji, they're the same square.
    • Kimono rental shops are everywhere if you want the full look.
    • Look for the golden Asahi flame building across the river.
  • Tembo DeckThe main observation deck at 350m, with a glass floor panel to stand over the drop.
    Tembo GalleriaA spiraling glass skywalk climbing to 450m, the highest point you can reach.
    Sunset timingGo up an hour before sunset and watch the city switch from gold to a sea of lights.
    SolamachiThe mall at the base, with an aquarium, food, and souvenirs if the weather turns.

    Good to know

    • Book a timed ticket to skip the long line.
    • The air is clearest in the morning or right after rain, best for the Fuji view.
    • Sunset is the prime slot and sells out first.
Tempura or sushi dinner nearby
Day3

The Tokyo From the Movies

Shibuya + Harajuku · Mon Aug 3

The young, loud, high-energy side of the city — from a quiet forest shrine straight into the chaos.

  • The great toriiTwelve-meter cypress gates mark the entrance, among the largest wooden torii in Japan.
    Sake barrel wallRows of donated sake barrels (and a matching wall of French wine) line the approach.
    Main shrineThe quiet central courtyard, where you can write a wish on a wooden ema plaque.
    Inner GardenA serene paid garden with an iris field and a spring, worth it in season.
    A wedding processionWeekends often bring a traditional Shinto wedding walking through, all in white.

    Good to know

    • It's free and opens with the sunrise.
    • Enter straight from Harajuku station.
    • Go early for the quietest, most magical version.
  • Takeshita StreetA pedestrian lane crammed with wild fashion, boutiques, and teenage energy.
    Rainbow treatsFoot-long cotton candy and overstuffed crepes, the street's signature snacks.
    OmotesandoThe elegant tree-lined avenue, called Tokyo's Champs-Elysees, with striking flagship architecture.
    Cat StreetA relaxed backstreet of vintage shops and cafes connecting Harajuku to Shibuya.

    Good to know

    • Takeshita is busiest and best on a weekend afternoon.
    • Cross to Omotesando when you want calmer, upscale browsing.
    • Great area to just walk and watch the style go by.
  • Scramble CrossingThe world's busiest crossing. Cross it, then watch it from above with a coffee.
    Hachiko statueThe bronze dog who waited years for his owner, Tokyo's favorite meeting spot.
    Shibuya SkyAn open-air rooftop deck 230m up, the best view straight down onto the crossing.
    Center-gaiThe pedestrian spine of shops, arcades, and cheap eats behind the crossing.
    Nonbei YokochoDrinker's Alley, a tiny lane of postage-stamp bars for later in the night.

    Good to know

    • The Starbucks and Shibuya Sky both give a great crossing view from above.
    • Nights are when it really glows.
    • Shibuya Sky sells timed tickets, sunset slots go first.
Dinner & nightlife in Shibuya
Day4

Arcade Heaven

Akihabara + Ueno · Tue Aug 4
FELIX'S FAVORITE

Whole buildings that are nothing but arcades, seven or eight floors each, wrapped in anime billboards and electronics stacked to the ceiling.

  • Multi-floor arcadesGiGO and Taito Stations, floors of claw machines, fighting games, and rhythm machines.
    Super PotatoThe legendary retro-game store, old Nintendo and Sega you can't find anywhere else.
    Yodobashi AkibaA giant of a electronics store, nine floors of everything with a plug.
    Anime megastoresAnimate and Mandarake for manga, figures, and collectibles.
    Gachapon hallsRooms wall-to-wall with capsule-toy machines, a few hundred yen a pull.

    Good to know

    • Budget a big chunk of the day, it's easy to lose hours here.
    • Weekends close the main street to cars, so you can walk it freely.
    • Bring coins for the arcades and gachapon.
  • Classic consolesFamicom, Super Famicom, Mega Drive and more, boxed carts stacked floor to ceiling.
    Rare importsHard-to-find titles and collector editions, the reason people fly in for this store.
    Retro arcade floorThe top floor is a working arcade of old cabinets, coins in the slot.
    Game merchSoundtracks, plush, and pixel-art souvenirs from the golden age.

    Good to know

    • Prices climb for boxed and rare, browse before you buy.
    • The upstairs arcade is a great break from shopping.
    • It's cramped and busy, go with a mission in mind.
  • Tokyo National MuseumThe largest and oldest museum in Japan, the country's art and history in one place.
    Nature & Science MuseumDinosaurs, a blue-whale model, and hands-on science, great with a teen.
    Ueno ZooJapan's oldest zoo, and home to the giant pandas.
    Shinobazu PondA lotus-covered pond with a shrine on an island in the middle.
    Ameyoko MarketA loud, cheap open-air market street of food, snacks, and bargains under the tracks.

    Good to know

    • Pick one or two museums, you can't do them all.
    • Ameyoko is the spot for a cheap, lively bite.
    • The park is big and shaded, a good midday breather.
Evening: themed cafe or Ameyoko street eats
Day5

The Reset — Onsen Overnight

Hakone · Wed Aug 5
CONFIRMED KEEPER

The total opposite of the go-go-go city days. The scenic Romancecar out from Shinjuku, then slow all the way down.

  • Hakone Tozan RailwayA tiny mountain train that switchbacks up through the forest and hydrangeas.
    OwakudaniA steaming volcanic valley of sulfur vents. Try a black egg boiled in the hot springs.
    Hakone RopewayA cable car gliding over the valley with Mt. Fuji ahead on a clear day.
    Lake Ashi cruiseA replica pirate ship across the lake, past the red torii standing in the water.
    Open-Air MuseumA hillside sculpture park with Picassos and Mt. Fuji as the backdrop.

    Good to know

    • The Hakone Free Pass covers the whole loop of trains, cable cars and boats.
    • Do the circuit in one direction so you never backtrack.
    • Overnight at a ryokan here for the full slow-down.
  • Tatami roomWoven-mat floors, low table, and a view. Slippers off at the door.
    Yukata robeYou change into a light cotton robe and wear it to dinner and the baths.
    Kaiseki dinnerA long, seasonal multi-course meal, often served in your room. The highlight of the stay.
    Private & public bathsOnsen baths to soak in before dinner and again before bed.
    OmotenashiThe quiet, anticipatory hospitality, your futon is laid out while you eat.

    Good to know

    • Dinner is usually included and is the main event, arrive by late afternoon for it.
    • Bathe before dinner, then soak again before sleep.
    • It's a splurge night, worth building the trip around one.
  • Wash firstYou shower and scrub at a seated station before you ever get in the bath.
    RotenburoThe outdoor baths, soaking in steaming water with a mountain or sky view.
    Private family bathMany inns offer a kashikiri, a private bath you can book if you'd rather not share.
    The small towelYou carry a little towel for modesty, but keep it out of the water, resting it on your head.

    Good to know

    • Baths are traditionally bare, no swimsuits, and split by gender.
    • Soak 10 to 15 minutes, rinse off, and drink water after.
    • Tattoos can be an issue at some baths, a private bath sidesteps it.
Stay the night · the full slow-down
Day6

Back to the City

teamLab + Ginza · Thu Aug 6

Romancecar back around midday, so a clean two-part day — one big afternoon, one nice evening. No running around.

  • BorderlessArtworks wander between rooms with no map and no route, you just get lost in it.
    PlanetsYou go barefoot and wade through water rooms and soft light, a full-body version.
    Lamp ForestA mirrored room of hanging lamps that light up in a chain around you.
    Floral projectionsFlowers bloom and animals form and scatter across the walls and floor in real time.

    Good to know

    • Book a timed ticket well ahead, it sells out.
    • At Planets you wade through water, so wear or bring shorts.
    • Give it two to three hours, it's bigger than it looks.
  • Ginza Six & flagshipsThe luxury department stores and brand flagships, with a rooftop garden up top.
    ItoyaA twelve-floor stationery paradise, weirdly one of the best stops in the district.
    SushiFrom hushed omakase counters to fast, excellent conveyor-belt spots.
    DepachikaThe department-store basement food halls, a feast just to walk through.
    KabukizaThe grand kabuki theater nearby, worth a look even from outside.

    Good to know

    • On weekend afternoons the main street goes car-free, walk right down the middle.
    • Great for browsing even on a budget.
    • A good, slower evening after a busy travel morning.
  • SushiFrom a splurge omakase counter to a spinning conveyor belt, both are worth doing.
    TempuraFeather-light battered seafood and veg, fried in front of you at a counter.
    TonkatsuA crisp, juicy breaded pork cutlet with rice and cabbage, comfort food done right.
    YakinikuGrill-your-own Japanese barbecue, a fun, hands-on dinner with a teen.
    IzakayaA Japanese pub, small plates and drinks, the most relaxed and social way to eat.
    KonbiniDon't sleep on convenience-store food, the sandwiches and snacks are genuinely great.

    Good to know

    • Top sushi and tempura counters need reservations, plan those ahead.
    • Izakaya are casual and perfect for father-son, order as you go.
    • Depachika food halls are ideal for grabbing dinner to go.
Easy after a travel morning

Day8

South on the Bullet Train

Tokyo → Kyoto · Sat Aug 8

Ekiben on the platform, 2h15 at 320 km/h, Mt. Fuji out the right-side window. You land mid-day — enough for a market lunch and a first walk through the old city.

  • Tokyo to KyotoThe Tokaido line runs it in about 2h15, city center to city center, no airport hassle.
    The Fuji sideReserve a seat on the right heading west and watch Mt. Fuji roll past the window.
    EkibenBeautiful boxed lunches sold on the platform, eating one on board is part of the ritual.
    Nozomi vs HikariNozomi is fastest, Hikari is covered by the rail pass, both get you there in comfort.

    Good to know

    • Sit on the right side going west for the Mt. Fuji view.
    • Grab an ekiben and a coffee before you board.
    • Be on the platform early, it leaves to the second.
  • Skewer crawlGrilled scallops, wagyu skewers, fried chicken, eat your way stall to stall.
    TamagoyakiWarm, sweet rolled omelet made in front of you, a market classic.
    Weird & wonderfulBaby octopus with a quail egg inside its head, tiny candied fish, pickle barrels.
    Matcha everythingSoft serve, warabi mochi and lattes, your first taste of Uji tea before the real thing.
    Knife shopsAritsugu has forged blades here since 1560, worth a look even if you don't buy.

    Good to know

    • Eat standing at the stall you bought from, walking and eating is frowned on.
    • Go hungry and order small everywhere.
    • Most stalls wind down by late afternoon, it's a lunch spot not a dinner one.
  • Hanamikoji StreetThe postcard lane of preserved teahouses, best in the early evening light.
    Shirakawa CanalA quieter, willow-lined stretch along the water, arguably prettier than the main drag.
    Yasaka ShrineThe bright vermilion shrine at Gion's end, its lantern-covered stage glows after dark.
    Pontocho AlleyAcross the river, a narrow lane of restaurants stacked over the water.
    Kaiseki or kushikatsuDinner in the district can be a splurge or simple skewers, both done well.

    Good to know

    • Dusk is the magic hour, time dinner around it.
    • Don't photograph geiko up close, side streets have fines posted.
    • Yasaka Shrine is free and open late, great after-dinner walk.
Check in · lanterns after dark
Day9

The Kyoto Postcard Day

Fushimi Inari + Higashiyama · Sun Aug 9

The two most famous sights in Japan, connected by the best old-town walk in the country. Start at sunrise to beat the heat and the crowds.

  • Senbon ToriiThe famous twin tunnels of gates right after the main shrine, the photo everyone comes for.
    The mountain climbGates continue all the way up Mount Inari, about 2 hours round trip if you go to the top.
    Yotsutsuji viewpointHalfway up, a wide view over Kyoto, most people turn around here and that's plenty.
    Fox statuesInari's messenger foxes guard every stage of the climb, keys and scrolls in their mouths.
    Street snacksThe approach street does grilled mochi and inari sushi, the fried tofu the foxes supposedly love.

    Good to know

    • Go at 7am or earlier, by 9 it's shoulder to shoulder in August.
    • The higher you climb the emptier the gates, photos get better with altitude.
    • It's a real hike in summer heat, bring water.
  • The main stageThe famous veranda hanging 13 meters over the hillside, the classic Kyoto view.
    Otowa WaterfallThree streams below the hall, drink from one for luck in studies, love or long life. Pick one, taking all three is greedy.
    Sannenzaka & NinenzakaThe two sloping stone lanes below the temple, wooden shops, tea houses and zero cars.
    Yasaka PagodaThe five-story pagoda rising out of the rooftops, Kyoto's most photographed skyline.
    Jishu ShrineThe matchmaking shrine on the temple grounds, walk between the love stones with your eyes closed.

    Good to know

    • Morning light is best on the main hall, afternoons get packed.
    • The lanes below are full of snack stops, save room.
    • It flows perfectly into an afternoon wander toward Gion.
  • The temple walkKiyomizu down through Sannenzaka, Ninenzaka, past Kodai-ji and into Gion, all on foot.
    Tea breakStop for matcha and a sweet in a machiya courtyard, the proper Kyoto pause.
    Craft shopsCeramics, folded knives, incense and yatsuhashi cinnamon sweets, souvenir central done tastefully.
    Maruyama ParkThe open park behind Yasaka Shrine, a shaded breather at the walk's end.
    Kimono spottingHalf the visitors rent kimono for this walk, it makes every photo better.

    Good to know

    • Do the walk downhill, Kiyomizu to Gion, it's all gentle descent.
    • Duck one lane off the main route and the crowds vanish.
    • August is hot, front-load the walking and take the afternoon slow.
Early start · slow afternoon
Day10

Deer, Buddha & Tea

Nara + Uji · Mon Aug 10

Japan's first capital is 45 minutes away: 1,200 wild deer that bow for crackers, and a 500-ton bronze Buddha in a colossal wooden hall. Uji, the tea capital, is on the train line home.

  • Nara Park deerBuy shika senbei (deer crackers) from a street cart and get mobbed by deer that literally bow to ask. Endless entertainment.
    Todai-jiThe Great Buddha Hall: a 15-meter, 500-ton bronze Buddha from the year 752 inside a colossal wooden hall.
    The pillar holeA pillar in the hall has a hole said to be the size of the Buddha's nostril, crawl through it for enlightenment. Mostly for kids, absolutely still funny.
    Kasuga TaishaA shrine reached through forest paths lined with 3,000 stone lanterns, deer wandering between them.
    NakatanidouThe famous high-speed mochi-pounding shop, catch the show and eat one warm.

    Good to know

    • Go morning for cooler air and calmer deer, they get pushy by afternoon.
    • Bow to a deer and it bows back, hide the crackers when you're done or they will find them.
    • The JR Nara line back to Kyoto stops at Uji, so tea town pairs perfectly with the return trip.
  • Byodo-inThe Phoenix Hall floating over its pond, the temple on the back of the 10-yen coin.
    Omotesando tea streetThe lane to the temple is wall-to-wall tea houses, matcha soft serve, tea-dusted everything.
    A proper matchaSit down for stone-ground matcha prepared in front of you at a centuries-old shop like Tsuen, the oldest tea house in the world.
    Uji River bridgeA wide green river with cormorant fishing boats and a bridge that's been rebuilt since the 600s.
    Matcha sobaGreen tea noodles for lunch, when in Uji.

    Good to know

    • It's on the JR Nara line, 20 minutes from Kyoto and on the way back from Nara, so it slots into the same day.
    • Byodo-in's interior tour is limited entry, the garden ticket alone is worth it.
    • Buy tea here, not at the airport, it's the real thing at half the price.
  • Hanamikoji StreetThe postcard lane of preserved teahouses, best in the early evening light.
    Shirakawa CanalA quieter, willow-lined stretch along the water, arguably prettier than the main drag.
    Yasaka ShrineThe bright vermilion shrine at Gion's end, its lantern-covered stage glows after dark.
    Pontocho AlleyAcross the river, a narrow lane of restaurants stacked over the water.
    Kaiseki or kushikatsuDinner in the district can be a splurge or simple skewers, both done well.

    Good to know

    • Dusk is the magic hour, time dinner around it.
    • Don't photograph geiko up close, side streets have fines posted.
    • Yasaka Shrine is free and open late, great after-dinner walk.
Bow to a deer — it bows back
Day11

Bamboo, Gold, then Osaka

Arashiyama + Kinkaku-ji · Tue Aug 11

Kyoto's grand finale in one sweep — bamboo forest early, the Golden Pavilion at midday — then a 15-minute bullet-train hop and you're in Osaka by dinner.

  • Bamboo GroveThe towering green corridor from every Japan photo set. Short, surreal, and best before the tour buses.
    Monkey Park IwatayamaA 20-minute hike to a hilltop where snow monkeys roam free and the view covers all of Kyoto.
    Togetsukyo BridgeThe Moon Crossing bridge over the Katsura River, the district's centerpiece.
    Tenryu-jiA World Heritage zen temple whose garden borrows the mountains as its backdrop, the bamboo grove exits from its north gate.
    Riverside lunchYudofu (hot tofu) is the local specialty, or grab unagi over rice.

    Good to know

    • Be at the bamboo grove by 8am, it's a different place before the crowds.
    • In the monkey park the humans are the ones behind the fence at feeding windows.
    • Rent bikes if the heat allows, the district spreads out.
  • The pavilionTwo floors of gold leaf topped with a phoenix, rebuilt in 1955 after a monk burned it down, a story worth reading.
    The mirror pondKyoko-chi, positioned so the pavilion reflects perfectly, the photo takes itself.
    The garden circuitA one-way stroll through the grounds, past the head priest's quarters and a small tea garden.
    Matcha stopThe tea house on the way out does matcha with a gold-flecked sweet, on theme.

    Good to know

    • It's a 30 to 40 minute visit, perfect between Arashiyama and the train to Osaka.
    • Sunlight on the gold is best mid-morning to midday.
    • Buses get slow here, a taxi from Arashiyama saves real time.
  • The Glico ManThe century-old running-man billboard over Ebisu Bridge, Osaka's mandatory photo.
    Street-food gauntletTakoyaki, okonomiyaki (the savory pancake Osaka perfected), kushikatsu fried skewers, grilled crab legs.
    Round1 DotonboriEight floors of arcade, claw machines, and on the Spo-Cha floors, batting cages and roller skating. Open basically all night.
    Canal cruiseA 20-minute boat run under the neon, cheap and surprisingly great after dark.
    Hozenji YokochoOne lane back, a stone alley with a moss-covered temple statue, old Osaka hiding behind the neon.

    Good to know

    • Come at dusk and stay into the night, the neon is the show.
    • Okonomiyaki etiquette: eat it off the griddle with the little spatula.
    • Kushikatsu rule every local will tell you: no double-dipping the sauce.
Kyoto out · Osaka in
Day12

The Big One

Super Nintendo World · Wed Aug 12
DEFINITE YES

Walk through a warp pipe and come out inside a life-size Mushroom Kingdom. Full day at Universal, midweek, when the park is at its calmest — which in August still means rope drop.

  • Mario Kart: Koopa's ChallengeAR headset kart racing through Bowser's Castle, the flagship ride and worth any wait.
    Power-Up BandsWristbands that link to the app, punch real question blocks, collect coins and keys, and compete against each other. Dad vs Felix, obviously.
    Donkey Kong CountryThe mine-cart coaster that appears to jump broken track, plus the whole jungle expansion.
    Yoshi's Adventure & Kinopio's CafeThe slow scenic ride with the best views of the land, and lunch inside a giant mushroom.
    The rest of USJHarry Potter's Hogsmeade, Jurassic Park, Hollywood Dream coaster, a full park beyond the pipe.

    Good to know

    • Buy studio passes ahead, and strongly consider the Express Pass with timed Nintendo entry, August is peak season. On sale roughly 2 months out, so now.
    • No Express Pass? Rope-drop: arrive before opening, go straight to Nintendo World, or grab a timed-entry slot in the app the moment you're through the gates.
    • Power-Up Bands are bought in-park, one each, they keep your scores separate.
